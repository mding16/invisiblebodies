Big tech’s increased marketing for concepts like “cloud” storage advance a narrative that software and code are somehow separate from the hardware that supports them.

However, a “full stack” understanding of labor behind models like word2vec calls for critical consideration of the computers, servers, and other electronic devices that power the model.

Coltan is a “critical raw mineral in high-tech manufacturing” that can be refined to form tantalum, a chemical used to produce tantalum capacitors “that manage the flow of current in electronic devices” like mobile phones, computers, game consoles, and camcorders. As of 2014, the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) produces 40% of the global coltan supply. This labor is performed in hazardous environments by over “40,000 children and teenage miners” from “remote villages and towns” in the Kivu province. According to civil society leader Faustin Kantanga, the illicit coltan mines are “rife with prostitution, sexually transmitted diseases and rape” and “those travelling long distances to smuggle coltan are also vulnerable to child traffickers and recruitment by armed groups.” The coltan supply chain also includes the labor of women who undertake “menial tasks such as sorting, gathering, and washing, and providing services to the mining community such as cooking, [and] running small businesses.” As Safiya Noble writes in "Algorithms of Oppression", “These aspects of software and hardware development are important, and decreased engagements of women and people of color in the high-tech design sector, coupled with increased marginalized participation in the most dangerous and volatile parts of the information and communication technology labor market, have high impact on the artifacts such as search results themselves.”
